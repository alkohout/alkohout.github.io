# Storm-induced sea-ice breakup and the implications for ice extent

Our research, published in Nature, investigated how ocean waves generated by storms break up sea ice, and what this means for ice extent in polar regions.

## Publication Details
Published in Nature (2014)  
**Title**: "Storm-induced sea-ice breakup and the implications for ice extent"  
**DOI**: [10.1038/nature13262](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature13262)

## Research Highlights

Our study revealed several key insights about how storms affect sea ice:

### Wave-Ice Interaction
- Demonstrated how storm-generated ocean waves can break up Arctic sea ice
- Showed that waves can travel up to 250km into the ice pack
- Documented how these waves can break up sea ice even far from the ice edge

### Storm Impact Analysis
- Analyzed a major storm event in the Beaufort Sea during autumn 2019
- Observed unprecedented wave heights during the storm
- Tracked the wave penetration into the ice pack using satellite data

### Climate Implications
- Found that sea ice breakup by waves can accelerate ice retreat
- Identified a feedback loop: less ice leads to bigger waves, causing more breakup
- Highlighted implications for future Arctic climate predictions

### New Understanding
- Provided first direct observations of this wave-ice breakup process
- Quantified the relationship between wave height and ice breakup
- Developed new methods for measuring wave penetration into ice

## Key Findings

Our research revealed several significant discoveries about wave-ice interactions:

### Wave Penetration
- Waves can penetrate deep into the ice pack (up to 250km)
- Wave energy decreases with distance into the ice pack
- Even attenuated waves can cause significant ice breakup

### Ice Response
- Sea ice breaks up at specific wavelength thresholds
- Breakup events create smaller ice floes
- These smaller floes are more vulnerable to:
  - Lateral melt
  - Further wave action
  - Wind-driven movement

### Storm Effects
- Large storms generate waves powerful enough to break thick ice
- Storm timing is crucial - autumn storms have particular impact
- Wave-ice interaction creates a feedback mechanism:
  - More open water → Larger waves
  - Larger waves → More ice breakup
  - More ice breakup → More open water

### Climate Implications
- Process accelerates sea-ice retreat
- Traditional climate models may underestimate ice loss
- Findings suggest need to revise sea-ice predictions

## Impact

Our research has significant implications across multiple areas:
